Stroke Prediction Form

Men typically have a higher risk of stroke than women, although women tend to have more severe outcomes and higher mortality rates post-stroke.



The risk of stroke increases significantly with age, especially after 55, due to the natural aging of the cardiovascular system.



High blood pressure is the most critical risk factor for stroke. It can damage blood vessels in the brain, making them more prone to rupture or blockage.



Conditions like atrial fibrillation and coronary artery disease increase the risk of blood clots, which can lead to a stroke.



Married individuals often have a lower risk of stroke, possibly due to better social support and healthier lifestyle habits encouraged by partners.



Occupations that are sedentary or highly stressful can contribute to higher stroke risk, while physically active jobs might reduce it.



Urban living might expose individuals to higher levels of pollution and stress, potentially increasing stroke risk, while rural areas might have less access to healthcare.



Elevated glucose levels, particularly in individuals with diabetes, can damage blood vessels, making strokes more likely.



High BMI, indicative of obesity, is associated with various risk factors for stroke, including hypertension, diabetes, and heart disease.



Smoking damages blood vessels, increases blood pressure, and reduces oxygen in the blood, all of which contribute to a higher stroke risk.
